One argument is drawn fron the representation of the personal character and qualifications of Moses.IN its most important features it is such as could never have been produced by a writer collecting the traditional reminiscenes or legends of a later age: not such even as might have been drawn by a younger comtemporary. 
 The wilderness between this land and the land of promise, the troubles, temptations and failings of the state of such life, the giving of the law to a new-born and emancipated people, the setting up of the ordinances of a holy religion, are all typical events, prefiguring others of a like nature, but of still grander and grander import.
